Protect our Kauri trees 🌲

Kauri trees are some of the oldest living organisms on Earth. Many are over 1,000 years old, and some giants like Tāne Mahuta have stood for nearly two millennia.

They once covered huge areas of the upper North Island, forming dense forests that supported birds, insects, fungi, and entire ecosystems.

Why Kauri Trees Matter

Kauri trees are a taonga (treasure) of Aotearoa. Their massive trunks and towering height make them one of the most iconic species in New Zealand. Kauri forests store huge amounts of carbon, help regulate water flow, and create unique acidic soil environments that support rare plants and fungi.

Kaitiakitanga — Guardianship

Kaitiakitanga is the Māori concept of caring for the natural world. It’s about protecting what is precious for future generations. Kauri have deep cultural significance, representing strength, resilience, and connection to the land.

Our Responsibility

Protecting Kauri is about preserving culture, history, and the natural identity of Aotearoa.

What is Kauri Dieback? 🦠

Kauri dieback is caused by a microscopic organism that infects Kauri roots and blocks water and nutrients. Once infected, a tree cannot recover.

The Disease
🧬

How the Disease Works

The pathogen, *Phytophthora agathidicida*, produces spores that move through soil and water. These spores infect the fine feeder roots first, slowly spreading into the larger root system. As the infection progresses, the tree loses its ability to transport water and nutrients, causing gradual decline.

The organism thrives in moist soil and can survive for years without a host tree, making it extremely difficult to eliminate once present.

⚠️

Signs of an Infected Tree

  • 🍂 Yellowing leaves and thinning foliage
  • 🩸 Bleeding gum at the base of the trunk
  • 🌿 Sparse crown and dead branches
  • 🪵 Cracked or dry bark

Symptoms often appear slowly, meaning a tree may be infected long before it looks sick. Early detection is crucial to protect surrounding trees.

🔁

How Does It Spread?

  • 🥾 Mud on shoes and boots
  • 🐾 Soil on dog paws and fur
  • 🚲 Dirt on bike tyres
  • 🛠 Machinery, tools, and vehicles

Even a teaspoon of soil can contain millions of spores. Human movement is the biggest cause of spread, which is why cleaning stations and track closures are so important.

📍

Where It Has Been Found

Kauri dieback has been detected in the Waitākere Ranges, Northland, Coromandel Peninsula, and parts of Auckland. Some forests have been closed to protect remaining healthy trees.

Monitoring teams regularly test soil samples to track the spread and identify new infection zones.

❌ There Is No Cure

The only way to protect Kauri is to prevent the spread of the disease.

How to Help Protect Kauri 💚

Small actions make a huge difference. Every visitor plays a role in protecting kauri forests.

4 Simple Steps You Can Take

🥿

Clean your shoes and gear

Before entering AND after leaving any kauri forest, scrub and wash off your footwear using the cleaning stations provided. Soil trapped in boot treads is one of the biggest causes of spread.

Make sure to clean bags, walking poles, and any equipment that may have touched the ground.

🚶‍♂️

Do not touch the roots

Kauri roots spread wide and shallow. Even walking near the base of a kauri tree can compact the soil, damage roots, and expose them to infection.

Always stay on marked tracks and boardwalks to avoid harming the root systems.

🐕

Keep pets away

Dogs and other animals can carry pathogens and soil that harm kauri. Keep pets on a leash and away from kauri areas.

Clean their paws thoroughly before and after your visit to prevent accidental spread.

📞

Report sick trees

If you spot signs of a sick kauri tree — yellowing leaves, bleeding gum, or thinning canopy — report it to DOC immediately.

Early reporting helps conservation teams respond quickly and protect nearby trees.

Every Action Counts

Your choices protect kauri for future generations.
